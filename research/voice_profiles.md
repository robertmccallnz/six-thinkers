# Voice and Vernacular Research: Six Thinkers

Research compiled to guide text-to-speech performance tuning. For Gramsci, Kropotkin, and Bakunin, no audio recordings survive — profiles are reconstructed from contemporaries' written accounts, biographers, and the rhetorical texture of their surviving speeches/writing. For Freire, Deleuze, and Graeber, abundant filmed footage exists and profiles draw directly on that record.

---

## 1. Antonio Gramsci (1891–1937)

### Voice profile
No audio of Gramsci survives, but contemporaries agree on a distinctive instrument: a small, physically frail, hunchbacked man (under five feet tall) with a "thick Sardinian accent, as thick as the Corsican accent of Napoleon or Stalin's Georgian" ([Luigi Barzini, NYRB](https://www.nybooks.com/articles/1967/09/28/a-founding-father/)). Sardinian Italian carries different vowel colouring and stress than mainland Tuscan-standard Italian, marking him immediately as an outsider-intellectual in Turin and Rome. Fellow militants stressed that despite the accent and his physical smallness, he had unusual personal magnetism when speaking one-to-one — closer to a patient, humane conversational register than a booming tribune's voice ([Tribune, "Remembering Antonio Gramsci"](https://tribunemag.co.uk/2023/04/remembering-antonio-gramsci-2)).

### Cadence and tempo
Two very different tempos define his life. In the *Ordine Nuovo* period (1919–1920) he is described as building factory-council arguments methodically, addressing workers as intellectual equals rather than talking down to them — deliberate, didactic pacing, not fiery haranguing ([Tribune](https://tribunemag.co.uk/2023/04/remembering-antonio-gramsci-2)). By contrast, his 1925 parliamentary speech against the Fascist "law on secret associations" is combative and quick, full of interjections and rebuttals traded live with hostile deputies — a sparring cadence very different from his patient pedagogical mode ([Gramsci, Speech to the Italian Parliament, 16 May 1925](https://www.marxists.org/archive/gramsci/1925/05/speech.htm)). In his final prison years his physical collapse changed his voice altogether: witnesses describe him "coughing up blood, hallucinating and babbling in Sardinian dialect" — a broken, halting, regressive cadence very unlike his intellectual prime ([John Foot, LRB](https://www.lrb.co.uk/the-paper/v16/n11/john-foot/households-of-patience)).

### Vernacular and word choice
Gramsci inverted the standard Left vocabulary of his day: rather than saying one must "go out to" or "go down to" the working class, his own vocabulary was to "go up to" the working class — reversing the usual condescending spatial metaphor ([Tribune](https://tribunemag.co.uk/2023/04/remembering-antonio-gramsci-2)). Former prison pupils recalled that around him "we did not feel that weight, that distance that a worker almost always feels when speaking with an intellectual" — his register was deliberately levelling, avoiding academic jargon in favour of concrete parable and analogy ([Tribune](https://tribunemag.co.uk/2023/04/remembering-antonio-gramsci-2)). His prison letters often take the shape of parables and allegory, partly to slip past censors, and show a "touch of lighthearted humour" even under duress ([Strange Matters review](https://strangematters.coop/antonio-gramsci-letters-from-prison-review/); [Parallel Texts, Letters from Prison](https://paralleltexts.blog/2016/05/08/letters-from-prison-by-antonio-gramsci/)). His famous coinages ("hegemony," "subaltern," "organic intellectual") are dense but were often unpacked in speech through homely illustration rather than abstraction.

### Rhetorical habits
Two modes: the teacher and the debater. As a movement organizer he built arguments slowly and collaboratively, treating listeners as "conscious, intelligent protagonists" rather than an audience to be moved emotionally ([Tribune](https://tribunemag.co.uk/2023/04/remembering-antonio-gramsci-2)). In Parliament he was sharp, quick to answer interruptions with irony and precise rebuttal rather than volume ([Marxists.org, 1925 speech](https://www.marxists.org/archive/gramsci/1925/05/speech.htm)). He was comfortable with long silences and slow-built logic in writing (the Prison Notebooks are famously indirect, allusive, note-like) — this suggests a speaker who thought before speaking and preferred precision to rhetorical flourish.

### TTS direction
- **Pitch:** low-mid; slight nasal Sardinian coloring, not a "trained orator" boom.
- **Tempo:** mid, slowing markedly for the "prison voice" register; quick and clipped for the parliamentary/debate register.
- **Tags:** insert `[pauses]` before key theoretical terms (as if choosing the word carefully); use ellipses for the halting, ill later-period voice; em-dashes for parliamentary interruptions/rebuttals.
- **Replacement phrasings:** Replace "the ruling class" with "the people who tell us how to think, before we even ask"; replace "in conclusion" with "so — comrades — where does that leave us"; add a rhetorical question at the end of a point rather than a flat statement ("So, what are we teaching, eh?").
- **Sources:** [Barzini, NYRB](https://www.nybooks.com/articles/1967/09/28/a-founding-father/); [Tribune](https://tribunemag.co.uk/2023/04/remembering-antonio-gramsci-2); [Gramsci's 1925 parliamentary speech, Marxists.org](https://www.marxists.org/archive/gramsci/1925/05/speech.htm); [John Foot, LRB](https://www.lrb.co.uk/the-paper/v16/n11/john-foot/households-of-patience); [Letters from Prison, Parallel Texts](https://paralleltexts.blog/2016/05/08/letters-from-prison-by-antonio-gramsci/).

## 2. Peter Kropotkin (1842–1921)

### Voice profile
Kropotkin spoke English with a heavy, unmistakable Russian accent his entire life in exile, mispronouncing common words in idiosyncratic ways (rhyming "own" with "town," "law" with "low") — vivid enough that fellow anarchists turned it into an in-joke ("the sluffter fields of Europe" for "slaughter fields") ([Zoe Baker, "Fun Kropotkin Facts," citing H.W. Nevinson](https://anarchozoe.com/2021/06/30/fun-kropotkin-facts/)). Eyewitnesses at his American lectures described him speaking "in a quiet voice with a heavy Russian accent," his words "tumbling out in any order," yet with such evident sincerity and kindliness that he held audiences regardless ([Kropotkin in America, Cambridge](https://www.cambridge.org/core/services/aop-cambridge-core/content/view/1D2C09B251469368997695D7376A807B/S0020859000006192a.pdf/kropotkin-in-america.pdf)). He himself admitted he had learned English from books, "pronounced it very badly," and had to communicate with his first Scottish landlady by writing notes ([Kropotkin's memoirs, quoted via Zoe Baker](https://anarchozoe.com/2021/06/30/fun-kropotkin-facts/)).

### Cadence and tempo
Not a flashy or fast talker. His comrade Stepniak described him: "He trembles with emotion; his voice vibrates with that accent of profound conviction... His speeches, although he cannot be called an orator of the first rank, produce an immense impression" ([Kropotkin in America, Cambridge](https://www.cambridge.org/core/services/aop-cambridge-core/content/view/1D2C09B251469368997695D7376A807B/S0020859000006192a.pdf/kropotkin-in-america.pdf)). Roger Baldwin's biographical sketch describes him lecturing "from notes and in an English strongly accented, in a professorial but very earnest style," steadily building his argument point by point rather than leaping to conclusions ([Baldwin, *The Life of Peter Kropotkin*](https://azinelibrary.org/approved/life-peter-kropotkin-1.pdf)). Emma Goldman, describing his conversation rather than platform speech, called it "the rapid current of his speech, the music of his voice, and his sparkling wit" — suggesting private conversation moved faster than his platform delivery ([Goldman, *Living My Life*](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/emma-goldman-living-my-life)).

### Vernacular and word choice
Formal, professorial, but warm — never vulgar, never patronizing. Roger Baldwin stressed he had "no concern for himself, no sense of leadership or position" whether addressing scientists or workers ([Baldwin](https://azinelibrary.org/approved/life-peter-kropotkin-1.pdf)). Rudolf Rocker contrasted Kropotkin's writing/speech directly with Bakunin's: Kropotkin's anarchist arguments were "structured logically" with "an elaborate description of the future anarchist society" rather than Bakunin's pathos-driven bursts ([Rudolf Rocker, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_Rocker)). He mixed scientific vocabulary (geography, biology, evolution) with plain moral argument — mutual aid, solidarity, the commune — never dropping into slogans.

### Rhetorical habits
A patient explainer, not a rouser. He built cumulative, evidence-based cases (drawing on his training as a geographer and naturalist) and welcomed audience questions after lectures, "plying him with questions at the close of each address" ([Baldwin](https://azinelibrary.org/approved/life-peter-kropotkin-1.pdf)). He treated audiences as students to be walked through a logical structure, not a crowd to be inflamed — the opposite temperament to Bakunin's, despite sharing anarchist conclusions.

### TTS direction
- **Pitch:** mid, warm, slightly nasal Russian-accented English.
- **Tempo:** slow-to-mid, deliberate; words should not rush even when the sentiment behind them is passionate — the emotion shows through vibration/warmth in the voice, not speed.
- **Tags:** use `[pauses]` between clauses of an argument being built step by step; occasional mild mispronunciation/rolled cadence flavoring; avoid contractions to preserve the "bookish/foreign" English quality.
- **Replacement phrasings:** Replace "in conclusion" with "and so, you will see, step by step, where this leads us"; replace colloquial contractions ("it's," "don't") with fuller forms ("it is," "we must not"); add a gentle direct address to the listener ("my friends, consider this").
- **Sources:** [Zoe Baker, "Fun Kropotkin Facts," citing H.W. Nevinson](https://anarchozoe.com/2021/06/30/fun-kropotkin-facts/); [Kropotkin in America, Cambridge University Press](https://www.cambridge.org/core/services/aop-cambridge-core/content/view/1D2C09B251469368997695D7376A807B/S0020859000006192a.pdf/kropotkin-in-america.pdf); [Roger Baldwin, *The Life of Peter Kropotkin*](https://azinelibrary.org/approved/life-peter-kropotkin-1.pdf); [Emma Goldman, *Living My Life*](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/emma-goldman-living-my-life); [Rudolf Rocker, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_Rocker).

## 3. Mikhail Bakunin (1814–1876)

### Voice profile
No recordings exist, but the written record is unusually vivid and consistent: a physically enormous man ("gigantic," "his giant bulk, his athletic figure, his great Rabelaisian face") whose voice matched his frame — described elsewhere as "imperious, like that of a man accustomed to command" even in old age ([M. Jourdain, "Mikhail Bakunin" (1920)](https://www.libertarian-labyrinth.org/bakunin-library/m-jourdain-mikhail-bakunin-1920/); [Vizetelly, *The Anarchists*](http://dwardmac.pitzer.edu/vizetelly/vizetelly2.html)). Herzen called him "that avalanche of a Bakunin" — a description of overwhelming, torrential vocal presence rather than precision ([Jourdain](https://www.libertarian-labyrinth.org/bakunin-library/m-jourdain-mikhail-bakunin-1920/)).

### Cadence and tempo
Contemporaries are unanimous that he was not technically a polished orator but an overwhelming one. Bernard Lazare's assessment: "he was a mediocre orator, whose words were tumultuous and confused, artless, lacking in elegance; but he was passionate" ([Bernard Lazare, "Mikhail Bakunin," Anarchist Library](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/bernard-lazare-mikhail-bakunin)). George Woodcock similarly describes his "massive unkemptness" impressing an audience even before he spoke, and his "persuasive oratory" working through sheer force rather than structure ([Woodcock, *Bakunin*, DiText](https://ditext.com/woodcock/6.html)). Marx, hostile but attentive, said "he spoke with an imperative tone" and "had no tolerance for the slightest contradiction," his debates "truly formidable" due to learning combined with a total lack of restraint in attack — he "was never able to debate without insults" ([Osugi Sakae, "Marx and Bakunin," quoting Marx](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/osugi-sakae-marx-and-bakunin)). This suggests rapid, torrential, interruption-prone speech that built to climaxes rather than measured argument — Isaiah Berlin described his rhetoric as "riding from climax to climax," "hortatory," "ironical," "always gay, always entertaining" but rarely precise ([Isaiah Berlin essay via eNotes](https://www.enotes.com/topics/mikhail-bakunin/criticism/criticism/isaiah-berlin-essay-date-1955)).

### Vernacular and word choice
Grand, sweeping, moralistic vocabulary — liberty, justice, tyranny, slavery, fraternity — deployed for inflammatory rather than descriptive effect. Berlin notes he used words "principally not for descriptive but for inflammatory purposes," a "great master of his medium" even though the ideas underneath were often "shallow" ([Berlin](https://www.enotes.com/topics/mikhail-bakunin/criticism/criticism/isaiah-berlin-essay-date-1955)). He coined memorable slogans, e.g. "*Tous pour chacun et chacun pour tous*" (all for each and each for all) — Berlin calls this "schoolboy jingle" phrase-making highly characteristic ([Berlin](https://www.enotes.com/topics/mikhail-bakunin/criticism/criticism/isaiah-berlin-essay-date-1955)). Not vulgar in a modern profane sense, but savage in insult — Marx notes "he was never able to debate without insults" ([Osugi Sakae](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/osugi-sakae-marx-and-bakunin)).

### Rhetorical habits
A provoker and agitator, not a patient explainer (the inverse of Kropotkin). He built arguments through repetition, escalation, and moral denunciation rather than logical chains — Berlin's "riding from climax to climax of rhetorical evidence." He had essentially no comfort with contradiction or silence: any pushback triggered further, louder assertion. He treated an audience as a force to be swept up and electrified rather than persuaded point-by-point.

### TTS direction
- **Pitch:** low, booming, chest-voice; imperious.
- **Tempo:** fast, torrential, sentences that build and overlap; minimal clean pauses — energy accumulates rather than resets.
- **Tags:** use CAPS or emphasis-tags for climactic words ("we want no more — NO MORE — patriotism"); em-dashes for self-interrupting escalation; avoid ellipses/hesitation tags almost entirely — he does not hesitate.
- **Replacement phrasings:** Replace "do not ask permission" with "WHY do you beg them for what is already yours"; replace calm declaratives with rhetorical demands ("Freedom? Freedom is not given — it is TAKEN"); insert a repeated word for emphasis in the Bakunin manner ("each other — do you hear me — each other").
- **Sources:** [M. Jourdain, "Mikhail Bakunin" (1920)](https://www.libertarian-labyrinth.org/bakunin-library/m-jourdain-mikhail-bakunin-1920/); [Bernard Lazare, Anarchist Library](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/bernard-lazare-mikhail-bakunin); [George Woodcock, *Bakunin*, DiText](https://ditext.com/woodcock/6.html); [Osugi Sakae, "Marx and Bakunin," quoting Marx's description](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/osugi-sakae-marx-and-bakunin); [Isaiah Berlin essay, eNotes](https://www.enotes.com/topics/mikhail-bakunin/criticism/criticism/isaiah-berlin-essay-date-1955).

## 4. Paulo Freire (1921–1997)

### Voice profile
Freire's Portuguese carried the soft, sing-song intonation typical of Recife/Pernambuco in Brazil's Northeast, and his English retained a distinct, strong Brazilian-Portuguese accent — filmed on record. In his 1996 farewell interview he speaks English gently, mid-pitch, unhurried ([Paulo Freire, "An Incredible Conversation," YouTube, 1996](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aFWjnkFypFA); [University of Pennsylvania repository copy](https://repository.upenn.edu/items/0d40efd1-8afa-406a-b79a-75a8be7e9093)). One first-hand account of meeting him notes he preferred to speak Portuguese but switched to English graciously when needed, laughing warmly at the interviewer's admission of only "two-year-old" Portuguese ([VCU/JSTAE interview account](https://scholarscompass.vcu.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1223&context=jstae)). A retrospective academic profile calls his voice "simplicity and outspoken," describing him as "joyful, enthusiastic and intelligent," his speech reflecting his thought "clearly and spontaneously" without losing dialectical rigor ([Sapere pedagogico journal profile](http://siba-ese.unisalento.it/index.php/sppe/article/download/18418/15718)).

### Cadence and tempo
Unhurried and conversational even in formal lecture settings — accounts describe him as reluctant to be the first to speak in a room, letting silence sit, and his classroom "performance" as "discreet" ([Revista Matrizes profile](https://www.revistas.usp.br/matrizes/article/download/192809/178860/533945)). He was, famously, "a man who spoke with his hands" — his verbal cadence was matched and often driven by gesture ([Revista Matrizes](https://www.revistas.usp.br/matrizes/article/download/192809/178860/533945)). His English carries the "intonations and patterns of his Brazilian" speech even when the vocabulary is fluent and academic — meaning stress patterns land on different syllables than a native English speaker's would ([VCU/JSTAE account](https://scholarscompass.vcu.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1223&context=jstae)). He treated dialogue itself as the pedagogical unit — his own theory insists education must be "conversational" rather than a one-way lecture "acting on" a passive listener ([Freire on dialogical pedagogy, YouTube transcript excerpt](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fQq5_fID9xc)).

### Vernacular and word choice
Central recurring vocabulary: "dialogue," "conscientização" (critical consciousness), "the banking model" of education (his term of contempt for rote instruction), "praxis," "the oppressed," "reading the word and the world" ([The World / PRI documentary interview on "A is for Angicos"](https://theworld.org/stories/2021/10/01/new-doc-highlights-paulo-freires-vision-education-tool-transformation-filmmaker)). Register is warm-academic rather than clinical — he speaks of love, humility, and hope as technical pedagogical terms, not just sentiment. Not vulgar; deeply humane and patient, even when discussing oppression and violence structurally. He self-consciously acknowledged and defended his accent: "please, don't push me that hard: it's just my accent" is a documented, quotable Freire line about his own English ([Education Unlimited blog, quoting Freire](https://learning21c.wordpress.com/2020/03/11/please-dont-push-me-that-hard-its-just-my-accent-paulo-freire-and-the-cosmopolitan-teacher/)).

### Rhetorical habits
A converser, not a lecturer, even when formally lecturing — his pedagogy explicitly rejects monologue "propagandistic slogans, manipulation, or condescension" in favor of dialogue that treats the student as co-thinker ([Red Menace podcast summary of *Pedagogy of the Oppressed*](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mpdMCeRcn5g)). He is comfortable with silence and with letting others speak first, described as rarely volunteering to be first into a conversation despite being the most authoritative person in the room ([Revista Matrizes](https://www.revistas.usp.br/matrizes/article/download/192809/178860/533945)). He builds arguments through questions bouncing back to the audience/interlocutor rather than assertions delivered outward.

### TTS direction
- **Pitch:** mid, soft, gentle — never harsh or declamatory.
- **Tempo:** slow-to-mid, with real pauses for thought, not filler; stress landing on non-native-English syllable patterns.
- **Tags:** frequent `[pauses]`, ellipses for warm, thinking-aloud hesitation ("to read the word... is not enough..."); soft laughter tag `[laughs warmly]` when self-deprecating about his accent or when illustrating with an anecdote.
- **Replacement phrasings:** Replace "you must" with a softer, dialogic invitation — "and so, together, we begin to ask..."; replace declarative endings with a question returned to the listener ("...and this is a question, no? What does it mean, to read the world?"); insert his real self-referential line about his accent when appropriate ("forgive me — it's just my accent").
- **Sources:** [Paulo Freire, "An Incredible Conversation," YouTube (1996 interview)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aFWjnkFypFA); [University of Pennsylvania repository](https://repository.upenn.edu/items/0d40efd1-8afa-406a-b79a-75a8be7e9093); [Revista Matrizes profile of Freire's classroom manner](https://www.revistas.usp.br/matrizes/article/download/192809/178860/533945); [The World/PRI, "A is for Angicos" documentary interview](https://theworld.org/stories/2021/10/01/new-doc-highlights-paulo-freires-vision-education-tool-transformation-filmmaker); [Education Unlimited, on Freire's accent](https://learning21c.wordpress.com/2020/03/11/please-dont-push-me-that-hard-its-just-my-accent-paulo-freire-and-the-cosmopolitan-teacher/).

## 5. Gilles Deleuze (1925–1995)

### Voice profile
Extensively documented on film in *L'Abécédaire de Gilles Deleuze* (filmed 1988–89 with Claire Parnet, broadcast posthumously from 1994–95). Reviewers consistently describe his voice as rocailleuse/caillouteuse — "stony," "pebbly," "raspy," rough-edged, an effect of decades of heavy smoking and later serious respiratory illness: "sa voix a des grondements qui arrachent à la toux suffocante... des ronronnements quasi-félins" (his voice has growls that wrench through suffocating coughing... near-feline purrs) ([L'Autre Quotidien review of the Abécédaire](https://www.lautrequotidien.fr/hebdo/2025/2/24/ryh789xk1wltckeulzeagjbq7vseor)). Le Monde's retrospective calls it "une voix... caillouteuse, écaillée, toujours prête à se fondre en un rire à la toux mêlé" (a voice, stony, flaking, always ready to dissolve into a laugh mixed with coughing) ([Le Monde, "L'alphabet déglingué de Gilles Deleuze"](https://www.lemonde.fr/archives/article/2004/03/11/l-alphabet-deglingue-de-gilles-deleuze_356337_1819218.html)). Another commentator recalls laughing while listening to "sa voix rauque et caillouteuse" (his hoarse, gravelly voice) as he made a philosophical point ([OpenEdition, "Deleuze-Guattari et la prise de parole"](https://journals.openedition.org/leportique/1373?lang=en)).

### Cadence and tempo
Famous for near-constant amusement while speaking — Le Monde notes his "façon de ne jamais cesser de rire en parlant" (way of never ceasing to laugh while talking) and his perpetually surprised expression, "pas du tout en représentation" (not performing at all) ([Le Monde](https://www.lemonde.fr/archives/article/2004/03/11/l-alphabet-deglingue-de-gilles-deleuze_356337_1819218.html)). A published viewing-notes commentary on the Abécédaire describes his tempo as variable within a single letter/segment: "Gilles is sometimes tired at the beginning and then, suddenly, he gets irritated, exasperated, wakes up, it picks up speed" ([vadeker.net notes on the Abécédaire](http://vadeker.net/humanite/philosophie/abecedaire_Gilles_Deleuze.html)). Academic literature on his style of speech and writing repeatedly invokes "the stutter" (*bégayer*) as his signature — not a clinical stammer, but a rhythmic, self-interrupting, exploratory mode of building a sentence that resists smooth syntax in favor of testing and modulating an idea as it's spoken ([Academic commentary, "Stuttering Conviction," Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/book/8144/chapter/153631885); [Deleuze Studies, "Three Ways of Speaking"](https://www.euppublishing.com/doi/abs/10.3366/dls.2016.0212)).

### Vernacular and word choice
Precise philosophical vocabulary (concept, plane, assemblage, deterritorialization, becoming) delivered conversationally rather than as jargon, often illustrated with concrete, even domestic examples (cats, alcohol, drinking "one more glass") ([Purdue Deleuze Seminars transcript notes on the Abécédaire](http://truthbeauty.s3.amazonaws.com/DeleuzeA-Z.pdf)). He riffs on Parnet's questions, sometimes mocking their phrasing gently before answering seriously. Recurrent self-referential humor about his age, illness, and reclusiveness ("are you nuts? ...I am old and society has let go of me") appears in his own words on why he agreed to be filmed at all ([Purdue Deleuze Seminars, ABC Primer Lecture 2](https://deleuze.cla.purdue.edu/lecture/lecture-recording-2-g-m/)).

### Rhetorical habits
Deleuze in the Abécédaire is fundamentally conversational and dialogic — Parnet asks, he muses aloud, often circling a term for a while before settling into a clear formulation, then breaking that formulation with laughter or a qualifying aside ("smiling, but moving uncomfortably... as if he were undergoing some ordeal") ([Scribd summary/commentary on the Abécédaire](https://www.scribd.com/document/630456234/Deleuze-Abe-ce-daire-english)). In seminars, his voice is at points "barely audible over the musical accompaniment" of a smoky, crowded room — suggesting a naturally soft, unamplified, intimate delivery even to large groups ([Scribd, quoting the trailer description](https://www.scribd.com/document/630456234/Deleuze-Abe-ce-daire-english)). He is comfortable with digression and self-correction, treating a question less as something to answer directly than as an occasion to think aloud.

### TTS direction
- **Pitch:** low-mid, gravelly, textured (smoker's rasp).
- **Tempo:** variable — slow and effortful at the start of a thought, accelerating as the idea takes hold, occasionally breaking into a laugh-cough.
- **Tags:** `[laughs]` and `[coughs]` liberally, especially mid-sentence; ellipses for the "stutter" quality where he circles back on a word before landing it; em-dashes for the moment he interrupts his own formulation to qualify it.
- **Replacement phrasings:** Replace "a book is a tool" with "a book — how to put this — a book is a little machine, you see, it's not a book of... representation"; replace clean declaratives with self-interrupted formulations ("what does it do, your book — that's the only question, not what does it mean [laughs] — never what does it mean"); use "you see" / "if you like" / "how to say" as filler tics.
- **Sources:** [L'Autre Quotidien, review of L'Abécédaire](https://www.lautrequotidien.fr/hebdo/2025/2/24/ryh789xk1wltckeulzeagjbq7vseor); [Le Monde, "L'alphabet déglingué de Gilles Deleuze"](https://www.lemonde.fr/archives/article/2004/03/11/l-alphabet-deglingue-de-gilles-deleuze_356337_1819218.html); [L'Abécédaire de GILLES DELEUZE: D comme Désir, YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tLlSRFLThYw); [vadeker.net viewing notes on the Abécédaire](http://vadeker.net/humanite/philosophie/abecedaire_Gilles_Deleuze.html); [Purdue Deleuze Seminars archive](https://deleuze.cla.purdue.edu/lecture/lecture-recording-2-g-m/).

## 6. David Graeber (1961–2020)

### Voice profile
Extensive video record (RSA talks, Charlie Rose, Malinowski Memorial Lecture, countless YouTube interviews). Friends' eulogies specifically single out "his funny voice," rumpled hair, and a laugh that "could scare seagulls off of their perch" ([NYRB tribute; "David Graeber: A Celebration of His Life," YouTube memorial](http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PZlXJ3YqkMA)). Native New York cadence — informal, warm, quick — described by colleagues as someone who could "speak to people just like normal humans" despite being "the rare genius academic," his delivery mischievous and boyish even in serious lectures ([Memorial video](http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PZlXJ3YqkMA)).

### Cadence and tempo
Fast, associative, and full of self-interruption. Recorded eulogies specifically note his "mid-sentence laugh" — he would start laughing partway through a sentence, "have to like take a deep breath and recover," and then continue ([Memorial video, timestamped commentary](http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PZlXJ3YqkMA)). His actual talk transcripts (e.g. the RSA "On Bullshit Jobs" lecture) are full of natural spoken filler — "um," "you know" — dropped mid-explanation as he reformulates a point in real time rather than delivering polished lines ([RSA Replay, "On Bullsh*t Jobs," YouTube transcript excerpt](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kikzjTfos0s)). This matches the brief's description of mid-sentence self-correction and parenthetical asides — Graeber routinely qualifies a claim immediately after making it ("I should emphasize, as you pointed out, the key thing here is...") ([SRSLY Wrong podcast transcript with Graeber](https://museum.care/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/SRSLY-WRONG-Bullshit-Jobs-W_-David-Graeber-Transcript.docx)).

### Vernacular and word choice
Plainspoken, deliberately anti-jargon academic register mixed with a sharp, sometimes profane wit — the very term "Bullshit Jobs" is his coinage and signature vulgarism, used matter-of-factly rather than for shock ([Graeber, "Bullshit Jobs," The Anarchist Library](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/david-graeber-bullshit-jobs)). He favors concrete illustrative examples over abstraction — "duct tapers," "taskmasters," "goons," "flunkies" are his own folk taxonomy for types of pointless work, explained through workplace anecdotes rather than theory-speak ([NPR, "Bullshit Jobs" segment](https://www.npr.org/transcripts/642706138)). Warm, funny, self-deprecating; unafraid of "like," "you know," and other spoken-register markers even in formal keynotes.

### Rhetorical habits
A converser and provoker rather than a lecturer, even at the podium — he builds an argument by piling up examples and anecdotes that build inductively toward a reframing (rather than starting from a thesis and proving it deductively). He treats an audience as collaborators to be nudged toward an "aha," using humor as the primary rhetorical lever, and appears entirely comfortable with an audience laughing at/with him mid-argument. He wrote and spoke explicitly in favor of "dialogic modes of thought" over monologue, reflecting how he ran his own talks ([Graeber, "Anarchy — In a Manner of Speaking," Anarchist Library](https://usa.anarchistlibraries.net/library/david-graeber-anarchy-in-a-manner-of-speaking)).

### TTS direction
- **Pitch:** mid, casual, unforced — not a "lecture voice."
- **Tempo:** fast-ish, New York quick, with sudden slow-downs for emphasis right before a punchline or reframe.
- **Tags:** `[laughs]` (often mid-sentence, cutting off the sentence itself — use an em-dash into the laugh tag); ellipses for the "um, you know" reformulating pause; parenthetical asides marked with em-dashes rather than full stops.
- **Replacement phrasings:** Replace "the economy" with "what they call — quote unquote — 'the economy'"; replace a flat declarative with a self-correcting aside ("most of what people call the economy — and I mean most of it, like, seriously most of it — is just people taking care of each other"); end on a blunt, deadpan profanity rather than a formal closer ("the rest — yeah — the rest is bullshit `[laughs]`").
- **Sources:** [NYRB, "David Graeber, 1961–2020"](https://www.nybooks.com/online/2020/09/05/david-graeber-1961-2020/); ["David Graeber: A Celebration of His Life," YouTube memorial](http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PZlXJ3YqkMA); [RSA Replay, "On Bullsh*t Jobs," YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kikzjTfos0s); [NPR, "Bullshit Jobs" transcript](https://www.npr.org/transcripts/642706138); [Graeber, "Bullshit Jobs," Anarchist Library](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/david-graeber-bullshit-jobs).

## Notes on source thinness

For **Gramsci, Kropotkin, and Bakunin**, no audio or film recordings survive; profiles above rely entirely on secondhand written accounts (contemporaries, biographers, and their own surviving speeches/letters as texts). Bakunin's profile is the richest of the three in terms of *descriptive* eyewitness testimony (Herzen, Marx, Woodcock, Bernard Lazare all left vivid character sketches of how he sounded and argued), while **Kropotkin's** record, though solid, leans more on secondhand summary (Baldwin, Goldman, Cambridge scholarly reconstruction) than direct quotation of his speaking cadence. **Gramsci's** case is thinnest of all for actual "sound" — there is good material on his accent and his written-argument structure, but almost nothing describing the timbre or pacing of his voice specifically (as opposed to his intellectual manner), since most sources are literary critics and biographers working from letters and party records rather than eyewitness listeners.

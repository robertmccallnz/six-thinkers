01 · The Kaupapa

Written for replies. Not likes.

Every post in this kit ends with either a question, a claim to disagree with, or a challenge to name something. That's what moves a post from impressions to a comment thread. Suggested launch cadence: one thinker per day + one toolkit post + one koha post — eight days, one loop.

X · Bluesky

Short and punchy

One thought per post. Ask for the reply, not the retweet. Bluesky rewards sincerity; X rewards edge.

Substack Notes

Half a paragraph

Reads like an aside from an essay. One hook, one question. Good for essayists warming up the audience.

Facebook

Longer captions

Lead with the human stakes, then drop the link. FB comment sections still work for real discussion.

LinkedIn

Workplace framing

Reframe the theory as a workplace or education argument. Skip the jargon. Let the theory sneak in.

01 · Hegemony

Gramsci

Power, hegemony, institutions, and counter-power from below.

X · hook 1
"The old is dying and the new cannot be born; in this interregnum a great variety of morbid symptoms appear." Gramsci wrote that in a fascist prison in the 1930s. Name the morbid symptom you're watching in 2026. I'll start: the algorithm is now the school.
X · hook 2
Hegemony isn't the boot on your neck. It's the voice in your head that says "well, that's just how things are." Where did you last catch yourself repeating a line that wasn't yours?
Substack Note
Gramsci is the thinker I hand to anyone who says "but people vote against their own interests, so what can you do?" His answer, from a fascist prison cell, was: you build counter-hegemony. Slowly. In every classroom, every union meeting, every group chat. You don't wait for the crisis to convert people; you build the culture that makes the crisis legible when it comes. Course 01 is live. What's one institution near you that's ripe for a war of position?
Facebook
Antonio Gramsci wrote most of his best work in a cell, watching Mussolini's Italy from the inside. His guards were told to "stop this brain from working for twenty years." They failed. Course 01 — Gramsci for Aotearoa — is now live. Seven lessons. Free to read. Free to remix. Tell me: where in Aotearoa do you see the fight for hegemony playing out right now?
LinkedIn
Antonio Gramsci's core insight: power isn't just held by force, it's held by consent — and consent is manufactured every day, in every classroom, newsroom, LinkedIn feed. If you work in comms, education, or leadership, you're a hegemony worker whether you like the term or not. The question is: whose hegemony are you building? Course 01 is free at the link.

Comment prompt: Name one "common sense" in your workplace or community that you think is quietly serving somebody else's interests.

02 · Cooperation

Kropotkin

Cooperation, survival, working-class care, and socialist practice.

X · hook 1
Darwin's bulldogs said nature was red in tooth and claw. Kropotkin, a Russian prince turned anarchist, went to Siberia and found the opposite: cooperation is the survival strategy. Species that share, last. Name a mutual aid group in your town. I'll boost it.
X · hook 2
Every time your neighbour lends you a drill instead of you buying one, that's Kropotkin. Every time a marae feeds the town after a flood, that's Kropotkin. The economy of care already exists. It's just uncounted.
Substack Note
Kropotkin's Mutual Aid is the book I hand to anyone who's been told "socialism is against human nature." He spent years in Siberia watching animals, then years in exile watching workers, and drew the same conclusion from both: the species that survive are the ones that cooperate. Course 02 is up. Tell me about a mutual aid whakapapa in your own whānau — the pot of soup, the working bee, the tangi that fed a hundred people on nothing.
Facebook
Peter Kropotkin was a Russian prince who threw away the title, went to Siberia, and came back convinced that cooperation — not competition — is what keeps species and communities alive. Course 02 — Kropotkin and Mutual Aid — is live. Seven lessons. Free. Comment with a mutual aid story from your own street and I'll compile the best ones into a follow-up post.
LinkedIn
The most productive teams I've ever seen weren't the "high-performers competing for stack rank." They were the ones quietly running on mutual aid — covering each other's shifts, sharing knowledge without gatekeeping, protecting the new hire. Kropotkin wrote a whole book about why. Course 02 is free at the link.

Comment prompt: What's the smallest act of mutual aid you've received this year that you'd like to name publicly?

03 · Anthropology

Graeber

Debt as a tool of power, work made meaningless by design, and how ordinary people run things without bosses.

X · hook 1
David Graeber's question was simple: if your job vanished tomorrow, would anyone notice? Not "would you get fired." Would the world notice? Answer honestly in the replies. No judgement. This is a research project.
X · hook 2
Debt isn't a moral failure. It's a technology of control 5,000 years old. The first prisons were debtors' prisons. The first revolts were debt cancellations. Every jubilee in history has been working-class people rewriting the ledger. Whose ledger are you living in?
Substack Note
Graeber gave us three gifts: Debt: The First 5,000 Years, which put the whole moral panic about "living within your means" in its proper historical place; Bullshit Jobs, which named what most of us already suspected about the modern workplace; and The Dawn of Everything, which blew up the story that hierarchy is inevitable. Course 03 pulls those three together for Aotearoa. What's the most bullshit job you've ever had — and did you know it while you had it?
Facebook
David Graeber died in 2020 but his questions still hit harder than most living pundits. Why does the economy demand meaningless work? Why does debt feel like a moral failing when a bank writing off billions doesn't? Why do we still act as if there's no alternative to bosses? Course 03 is now live — Debt, Bullshit Jobs, Direct Democracy. Free. Tell me: what's a decision that got made about your workplace this year that you would have made differently if anyone had asked?
LinkedIn
"Bullshit Jobs" isn't an insult, it's a diagnosis. Graeber defined it as work so pointless the person doing it can't justify its existence — but has to pretend otherwise to keep the paycheque. He found it wasn't rare. It was structural. If you've ever sat in a meeting and thought "this could have been an email, and the email could have been nothing," you already understand the theory. Course 03 is free.

Comment prompt: What's a piece of "financial common sense" you were raised with that you now think was propaganda?

04 · Pedagogy

Freire

Paulo Freire's pedagogy of the oppressed, refitted for the age of generative AI and Aotearoa's own kura kaupapa Māori.

X · hook 1
Freire called it the "banking model" of education: the teacher deposits knowledge, the student is the vault, the vault gets tested. ChatGPT is now the biggest bank in history. Who's the depositor and who's the vault?
X · hook 2
Every algorithm is a curriculum. It's teaching you what to want, what to fear, what to click. You didn't consent to enrolment. You can still drop out.
Substack Note
Paulo Freire wrote Pedagogy of the Oppressed in exile, after being jailed for teaching Brazilian peasants to read. His crime was that reading, done properly, is dangerous. Course 04 puts Freire in conversation with kura kaupapa Māori and the generative AI moment — because the banking model of education has a new bank, and it's trained on the whole internet. What was the moment you realised your own schooling had been designed to produce a specific kind of worker?
Facebook
If you've ever felt like school was a factory that mostly taught you how to sit still and not ask why — you weren't imagining it. Paulo Freire wrote a whole book explaining the design. Course 04 — Freire mō Aotearoa: Pedagogy of the Algorithm — lands soon. It puts Freire's method next to kura kaupapa Māori and asks: what does "conscientisation" mean when your teacher is now a chatbot? What's one thing school taught you that you had to unlearn as an adult?
LinkedIn
Every L&D programme is a pedagogy. The question is which one. Freire's distinction — between "banking" education that deposits content into passive learners, and "problem-posing" education that treats learners as co-investigators of their own reality — is the single most useful frame I've found for designing learning that actually sticks. Course 04 is coming. Free to remix under CC BY-SA 4.0.

Comment prompt: What's the one lesson your schooling refused to teach you that you had to find on your own?

05 · Rhizome

Deleuze

Rhizomes, assemblages, and lines of flight — Deleuze's ideas for those who teach, create, and organise.

X · hook 1
A tree has a trunk. Chop it and it dies. A rhizome has no centre. Chop it anywhere and it grows sideways. Deleuze asked: is your movement a tree or a rhizome?
X · hook 2
"A line of flight is not an escape. It's what you become when you stop pretending the map is the territory." — reading Deleuze on a Tuesday What's a line of flight you're currently on?
Substack Note
Deleuze is the thinker people bounce off the hardest, and it's usually because someone handed them A Thousand Plateaus without a life-jacket. Course 05 is the shortest — one long lesson for now — because I want to test whether the rhizome, the assemblage and the line of flight actually land for people who teach, create and organise, before I build out the rest. Tell me: does "rhizome" click for you as a metaphor for organising, or does it slide off?
Facebook
If you've ever tried to describe a movement that has no clear leader, no single headquarters, no manifesto anyone fully agrees on — but somehow keeps growing — you're already thinking rhizomatically. Deleuze gave us the vocabulary. Course 05 is a taster: one lesson, free, testing whether these ideas earn their keep in organising work. Read it and tell me: tree, rhizome, or something in between?
LinkedIn
"Rhizome" is the most useful org-design metaphor I've come across, and it's not from a management book. Gilles Deleuze — with Félix Guattari — described networks that grow without a centre, resist top-down capture, and mutate faster than any hierarchy can respond. If your team is drawing an org chart, you're drawing a tree. If your team is drawing a map of relationships, you might be closer to a rhizome. Course 05 is a first taste.

Comment prompt: Name a rhizome you're part of — a group, a scene, a network — that has no clear boss but somehow keeps going.

06 · Federation

Bakunin

Federalist, anti-authoritarian socialism — the man Marx feared, read for Aotearoa.

X · hook 1
Marx wanted a workers' state. Bakunin said any state would eat the workers. A century and a half later, we've run both experiments. Tell me who was closer.
X · hook 2
Bakunin: "If you took the most ardent revolutionary, vested him in absolute power, within a year he would be worse than the Tsar himself." Every generation has to relearn this the hard way.
Substack Note
Bakunin is the ghost at every socialist wedding. Marx feared him, purged him, and history mostly remembered Marx — but every time a workers' state turns on the workers, Bakunin's warning gets re-read. Course 06 — Bakunin mō Aotearoa — reads him next to tino rangatiratanga, federation from below, and the whakapapa of hapū-based governance that never needed a Westminster to legitimise it. What does "federation from below" look like in your rohe?
Facebook
Mikhail Bakunin was the socialist the other socialists tried to erase. Why? Because he was the one who saw, in the 1870s, exactly what would happen if you built a revolution around a central state instead of federated communities. Course 06 puts Bakunin next to Aotearoa's own whakapapa of federated hapū governance — Tangi o te Tāheke, the lament of the cascade. Free. Tell me: what does "power from below" look like where you live?
LinkedIn
There's a version of leadership that centralises decisions and calls it "clarity." There's another that federates them, trusts the people closest to the work, and calls it "subsidiarity." Bakunin was arguing for the second one 150 years before it became a management buzzword. Course 06 makes the case, in plain language, that federation isn't utopian — it's just older, more tested, and more resilient than the alternative.

Comment prompt: Where in your life do you already practise federation without calling it that?

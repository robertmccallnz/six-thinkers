# Six Thinkers: Dialogues — A Writers' Track

## What this course is

This is not a lecture series and it's not art school. Six Thinkers: Dialogues takes the same six thinkers from the original Six Thinkers course — Gramsci, Kropotkin, Bakunin, Freire, Deleuze, Graeber — and turns each one into raw material for your own writing. No essay to memorise, no theory to recite back. You read a thinker in one paragraph, you watch them argue with someone else in the room, and then you take the argument apart yourself: an essay, a scene, a letter, a monologue, a list-piece for your Substack.

The premise is simple. Theory that stays locked in academic prose is theory that never gets used. Gramsci wrote from a prison cell, not a lecture hall. Kropotkin wrote for workers organising without bosses. Freire taught peasants to read by starting with their own lives, not a primer written by someone who'd never met them. This course treats their ideas the way they were meant to be treated — as tools you pick up, test against your own life, and put back down changed.

Each module ends where an essay should begin: with a live argument, a structural template, and a set of questions sharp enough to start a fight in a good way. You're not here to become an expert on Gramsci. You're here to write something true using Gramsci as a lever.

## Who it's for

This is for writers, essayists, playwrights, Substack publishers, and reading circles — anyone who thinks with a pen rather than a canvas. It's for the person who wants to write about the housing crisis but needs a frame sharper than outrage. It's for whānau and workmates running a reading circle in a lounge or a union hall, not a seminar room. Independent thinkers without institutional backing are the target audience, not an afterthought. If you're an art school student, you're welcome too — but nothing here assumes a studio or a critique panel. It assumes a laptop, a notebook, or a voice memo app, and the will to argue something out loud.

## How to use it

**Solo track.** Read the thinker sketch, sit with the dialogue seed, then pick one writing prompt. Don't do all five — pick the one that scares you slightly. Draft it in one sitting if you can. Come back to the dialogue frameworks when you're stuck on structure, not before.

**Reading-circle track.** One module a week. Someone reads the thinker sketch and the dialogue seed aloud. Then work through the reading-circle questions — they're built for people without a university behind them, and they're built to land on something in your own kāinga, workplace, or union. End the session by setting one writing prompt as homework, and read the results aloud next time before moving to the new module.

## The six-week rhythm

- **Week one** — Gramsci: hegemony as pedagogy. Who taught you what "normal" means, and who profits from it.
- **Week two** — Kropotkin: mutual aid as method. What you already do for free that the market calls impossible.
- **Week three** — Bakunin: refusal and the state. Where you draw the line and what happens when you do.
- **Week four** — Freire: reading the world. What your own life already taught you before any book did.
- **Week five** — Deleuze: books as machines, rhizomatic writing. Why your essay doesn't need a straight line.
- **Week six** — Graeber: care work, debt, bullshit jobs. What you're owed and what owes you nothing.

Run it faster or slower. There's no cohort to keep pace with. The only deadline is the one you set for your own writing.

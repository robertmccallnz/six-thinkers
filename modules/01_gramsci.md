# Gramsci: Hegemony as Pedagogy

## The thinker in one paragraph

Antonio Gramsci was an Italian Marxist who spent the last eleven years of his life in one of Mussolini's prisons, writing on scraps of paper because the fascist state had decided his ideas were dangerous enough to bury alive. He never got to finish a clean theory — the *Prison Notebooks* are fragments, arguments with himself, half-thoughts choked by censorship and failing health. What he left behind was hegemony: the idea that ruling classes don't just control you with police and prisons, they win your consent first, through schools, churches, media, the stories a society tells about what's "normal" and "natural." He didn't romanticise the working class as automatically revolutionary — he thought people had to be organised and educated into understanding their own conditions, which is why he mattered to teachers and organisers as much as to political theorists. He wasn't a saint. He made his peace with vanguard politics and centralised party discipline in ways that later got weaponised by people with far less integrity than him. Read him for the diagnosis, argue with him about the cure.

## The dialogue seed

**Gramsci and Freire, on the NZ curriculum, 2026.**

GRAMSCI: Your literacy circles start with the peasant's own word for hunger. Mine start with asking who wrote the textbook and who they wrote it for.

FREIRE: Same house, different door. You want them to see the hegemony first. I want them to see themselves first — as people capable of naming their own world. The naming comes before the seeing.

GRAMSCI: Naming without structural analysis is just therapy. Your NCEA student can name their landlord and still vote for the party that protects landlords.

FREIRE: And your student can analyse hegemony perfectly and still believe they're too stupid to speak in the room. Consciousness isn't a lecture, compañero. It's dialogue, and dialogue starts with dignity, not diagnosis.

GRAMSCI: Dignity without organisation is a Sunday feeling. What happens Monday, when the curriculum review board is stacked with the same three private-sector consultants who wrote the last one?

FREIRE: Monday, the student who found their own word for injustice is harder to silence than the one who memorised yours.

## Writing prompts

1. **Essay** — Pick one thing you were taught in school as simply "the way things are" (queue at the bank, private landlords, a forty-hour week). Trace who benefits from you never questioning it.
2. **Scene** — Write a staffroom conversation between a teacher who wants to "just teach the syllabus" and one who's started asking whose interests the syllabus serves.
3. **Letter** — Write a letter to a politician who talks about "common sense" policy, naming exactly whose common sense it actually is.
4. **Monologue** — Voice someone who has just realised the thing they were taught to be ashamed of (their accent, their benefit, their state house childhood) was never a personal failing but a manufactured story.
5. **List-piece** — List ten phrases you hear on the news that function as common sense, and name the interest hiding behind each one.

## Dialogue frameworks

1. **The pupil interrupts** — A teacher or authority is mid-lecture when a student breaks the frame and asks who benefits from the lesson being taught this way. Good for essays that want to break their own throat-clearing.
2. **The two rooms** — Cut between a boardroom where policy gets made and a kitchen where its effects land. Never let the two rooms speak directly — let the reader supply the connection.
3. **The manual rewritten** — Take an official document (a tenancy agreement, a school newsletter, a WINZ letter) and rewrite it line by line with the hidden interest spoken aloud in brackets.

## Reading-circle questions

1. What's one "common sense" idea in your own life that, on reflection, mostly benefits your boss or your landlord?
2. Gramsci distinguished a "war of position" (slow cultural struggle) from a "war of manoeuvre" (direct confrontation). Where do you see each happening in Aotearoa right now?
3. Who decides what counts as "balanced" reporting on the news you watch, and whose balance is it?
4. Gramsci wrote about "organic intellectuals" who come from the working class rather than above it. Who plays that role in your own community — the marae, the union branch, the tenants' union?
5. How does Pākehā "common sense" about property and Te Tiriti o Waitangi function as hegemony — and what would it take to make a different common sense feel natural instead?

## Further reading

- [Antonio Gramsci, Selections from the Prison Notebooks (excerpts)](https://www.marxists.org/archive/gramsci/prison_notebooks/) — the primary text, free to read.
- [Antonio Gramsci Archive](https://www.marxists.org/archive/gramsci/index.htm) — letters, early writings, and notebook selections collected together.
- Peter Mayo, *Gramsci, Freire and Adult Education: Possibilities for Transformative Action* — the clearest bridge between this module and the next.
- Kate Crehan, *Gramsci's Common Sense: Inequality and Its Narratives* — a recent, readable account of hegemony for people without a Marxist theory background.

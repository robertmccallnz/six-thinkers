# Bakunin: Refusal and the State

## The thinker in one paragraph

Mikhail Bakunin was the loudest and most combative voice in nineteenth-century anarchism, a Russian aristocrat turned revolutionary who spent years in tsarist and Austrian prisons before escaping across Siberia and landing in the middle of every uprising Europe had to offer. He fought Marx directly, inside the First International, over a single question that still splits the left today: if you seize the state to abolish class, who's to say the people who seize it won't just become the new ruling class? Bakunin thought the state was not a neutral tool that could be picked up by the working class and then discarded once its job was done — he thought the state was the problem, structurally, permanently, no matter whose hands held it. He predicted, with uncomfortable accuracy, that a "dictatorship of the proletariat" run by a party would become a dictatorship over the proletariat. He was not a careful theorist — his prose is furious, sometimes sloppy, occasionally ugly with the antisemitism that stains his legacy and deserves naming rather than excusing. Read him for the sharpest critique of state power the left ever produced, and don't let anyone launder his worst instincts out of the record while you're at it.

## The dialogue seed

**Bakunin and Deleuze, on platform capitalism, 2026.**

BAKUNIN: You call it a rhizome. I call it a new master with better manners. Facebook, Google, the algorithm — they don't need a police force when they can just shape what you're allowed to think is possible.

DELEUZE: You're still looking for a single throne to burn down, Mikhail. There isn't one. Power today is a network, distributed, capillary — it moves through desire, not decree. You can't storm a server farm and expect the machine to stop.

BAKUNIN: Then your rhizome is just a prettier word for surrender. If there's no centre, there's no target, and if there's no target, the people never learn to refuse anything at all.

DELEUZE: Refusal doesn't have to look like a barricade. A rhizome can be cut at any point and grow back differently — that's not weakness, that's the only kind of resistance that actually survives a networked enemy.

BAKUNIN: Pretty theory for people who've never had to actually choose a side. At some point you delete the account, quit the platform, build the alternative, or you don't. The middle ground you're describing looks a lot like the platform winning by default.

## Writing prompts

1. **Essay** — Name one institution in your life you've quietly stopped believing has legitimate authority over you, and argue for why.
2. **Scene** — Write a union meeting where one member argues for working inside the system and another argues the system itself is the obstacle.
3. **Letter** — Write a letter of resignation — real or imagined — from a job, a party, or a church, that explains exactly which authority you're refusing and why.
4. **Monologue** — Voice someone mid-way through a WINZ appointment, silently cataloguing every small surrender of dignity the process demands.
5. **List-piece** — List every form you've had to fill out this year that asked you to prove your right to exist in some official's eyes, and note who wrote the form.

## Dialogue frameworks

1. **The letter never sent** — A character drafts a furious letter of refusal to a landlord, employer, or minister, and the piece lives entirely in what they wanted to say versus what they eventually did.
2. **The vote that changes nothing** — Two characters argue before an election or referendum about whether working inside a system can ever undo what the system was built to protect.
3. **The last straw, narrated backwards** — Start with the moment of refusal — walking out, quitting, saying no — and work backwards through every smaller compromise that led there.

## Reading-circle questions

1. What's one form of authority in your life — landlord, employer, state agency — that you comply with daily without ever having agreed to its legitimacy?
2. Bakunin thought seizing state power just relocates domination rather than ending it. Do you see that pattern in any revolution or reform you've followed in your lifetime?
3. Where does refusal show up in Aotearoa union history — a strike, an occupation, a boycott — and what made it work or fail?
4. Is there a difference between refusing an unjust law and refusing the state itself? Where do you personally draw that line?
5. How does Bakunin's suspicion of centralised power connect to tino rangatiratanga and iwi resistance to a colonial state that has never asked permission to govern?

## Further reading

- [Mikhail Bakunin, God and the State](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/mikhail-bakunin-god-and-the-state) — his most widely read text, free online.
- [Mikhail Bakunin, Statism and Anarchy (excerpts)](https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/bakunin/works/statism-anarchy/index.htm) — the core of his argument against Marx.
- Mark Leier, *Bakunin: The Creative Passion* — a biography honest about both his brilliance and his ugliness.
- Ruth Kinna, *Anarchism: A Beginner's Guide* — solid context for placing Bakunin against Kropotkin and later anarchist thought.

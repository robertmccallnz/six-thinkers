# Deleuze: Books as Machines, Rhizomatic Writing

## The thinker in one paragraph

Gilles Deleuze was a French philosopher who, mostly working with the psychoanalyst Félix Guattari, tried to break open every tidy, tree-shaped model of thought that Western philosophy had settled into — the idea that ideas grow from a single root, branch in an orderly way, and can be traced back to one origin. Against that, he offered the rhizome: a tangled root system like ginger or couch grass, with no centre, no fixed hierarchy, connections running in every direction, capable of being cut at any point and growing back somewhere else entirely. He described a book not as a message to decode but as a machine — you don't ask what it means, you ask what it does, what it connects to, what it makes possible when it collides with your own life. This is genuinely difficult, sometimes deliberately so, and there's a real risk of using Deleuze as decoration — dropping "rhizomatic" into a grant application to sound clever. He's most useful to a writer not as a vocabulary flex but as permission: permission to write in fragments, to refuse a single thesis statement, to let an essay branch sideways if that's where the thinking actually goes.

## The dialogue seed

**Deleuze and Bakunin, on platform capitalism, 2026.**

DELEUZE: Every login you make is a small machine plugging into a larger one — you, the platform, the advertiser, the data broker. None of them are in charge. That's what makes it so hard to fight.

BAKUNIN: Convenient theory for whoever owns the servers. If no one's in charge, no one's responsible, and the people renting out your attention forever get to hide behind your beautiful diagram.

DELEUZE: I'm not saying no one profits. I'm saying the old model of storming the palace doesn't work when the palace is a distributed network with no single door.

BAKUNIN: Then find the new doors. Data centres exist. Boards exist. Founders exist. Your rhizome has roots you can still dig up, Gilles, you're just reluctant to get your hands dirty.

DELEUZE: Or I've noticed that cutting one root doesn't kill the plant — the network reroutes around you unless the resistance is itself networked, distributed, plural. Not a single revolution. A thousand small refusals, connected sideways.

BAKUNIN: A thousand small refusals is what people tell themselves when they've stopped believing a big one is possible.

## Writing prompts

1. **Essay** — Write an essay with no single thesis — let it branch into three unrelated threads (a memory, a policy, an object) and trust the connections to surface without you stating them outright.
2. **Scene** — Write a scene with no clear protagonist, where the "action" is really just several small systems (a phone, a bus timetable, a workplace roster) quietly shaping a character's day.
3. **Letter** — Write a letter that refuses to resolve — no conclusion, no ask, just an honest tracing of connections between things that don't obviously belong together.
4. **Monologue** — Voice someone trying to explain their life and failing to make it into a straight-line story, because it never was one.
5. **List-piece** — List ten unrelated things that happened to you this week and find the underground root system connecting them without forcing a moral.

## Dialogue frameworks

1. **The argument overheard on the bus** — Two strangers' conversation drifts across three unrelated topics that turn out to share a root system the writer, not the characters, makes visible.
2. **The map with no centre** — Structure a piece as a set of linked fragments that can be read in more than one order, each fragment a node rather than a step in a sequence.
3. **The machine, not the message** — Take an object (a phone, a form, a fence) and describe only what it does — what it connects, blocks, enables — never what it "means."

## Reading-circle questions

1. Deleuze rejects the idea that thought has to grow from one root. Where in your own thinking have you forced a straight line onto something that was actually tangled?
2. What would it mean to read a policy document, a Bible, or a union contract as a "machine" — asking what it does rather than what it claims to mean?
3. Platform capitalism is often described as a network with no centre. Does that make it harder or easier to organise against?
4. Is fragmented, rhizomatic writing a genuine tool for people without institutional power, or a style that only works once you already have an audience?
5. How does a rhizomatic, non-hierarchical model of connection compare with whakapapa — which is also relational and non-linear, but carries a structure and genealogy Deleuze's model doesn't account for?

# Graeber: Care Work, Debt, Bullshit Jobs

## The thinker in one paragraph

David Graeber was an American anthropologist, an active organiser inside Occupy Wall Street, and one of the sharpest and funniest writers the modern left produced before his sudden death in 2020. He built his arguments from actual fieldwork and history rather than pure theory: *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* dismantles the myth that money began as a neutral solution to the awkwardness of barter, showing instead that debt has always been a technology of obligation and control, often backed by violence. *Bullshit Jobs* took a simple observation — that huge numbers of people privately believe their own jobs are pointless — and turned it into a serious argument about how capitalism generates meaningless work to keep people compliant and exhausted, while genuinely useful work like caregiving and cleaning gets paid the least. He was an anarchist who believed direct action and consensus-based organising weren't a rehearsal for some future revolution but the revolution already happening in miniature, wherever people organise without waiting for permission. He could be glib, and some of his historical claims have been challenged by specialists — read him as a brilliant provocateur, not gospel, and check his sources when you cite him.

## The dialogue seed

**Graeber and Kropotkin, on the care economy, 2026.**

GRAEBER: A rest home carer in Rotorua gets paid nineteen dollars an hour to keep someone alive. A hedge fund analyst gets paid four hundred thousand to move numbers between accounts that produce nothing. That's not a market failure. That's the market working exactly as designed.

KROPOTKIN: Which is why the carer shouldn't wait for the market to notice her value. Build the cooperative rest home, owned and run by the workers and the families, and cut the shareholder out entirely.

GRAEBER: I agree with the instinct, but debt is the leash that keeps her from walking away and building that cooperative. She owes rent, owes the power company, owes on a car she needs to get to a job she can't afford to lose. The bullshit economy doesn't just exist above her — it's wrapped around her ankles.

KROPOTKIN: Then mutual aid has to include debt refusal — rent strikes, debt jubilees, collective bargaining that treats debt itself as illegitimate, not just low wages.

GRAEBER: Now you're talking my language. Five thousand years of history says debt forgiveness isn't radical, it's how most societies reset before the whole thing collapsed under its own extraction. We're just refusing to have that conversation out loud.

## Writing prompts

1. **Essay** — Interview (real or composited) someone in a genuinely useful, poorly paid job — carer, cleaner, teacher aide — and one in a well-paid job they privately consider pointless, and lay the two accounts side by side.
2. **Scene** — Write a scene at a bank or WINZ office where a debt conversation reveals more about power than about numbers.
3. **Letter** — Write a letter from a care worker to the CEO of the company that employs her, itemising exactly what her nineteen dollars an hour actually buys the company.
4. **Monologue** — Voice someone describing their job to a child, and struggling to explain what it actually produces.
5. **List-piece** — List every debt you currently carry or have carried, and beside each one note who profits from you continuing to owe it.

## Dialogue frameworks

1. **The argument overheard on the bus** — Two shift workers debate whether their jobs matter, overheard by a narrator who supplies the wider economic picture neither of them has access to.
2. **The ledger turned inside out** — Structure a piece as a literal ledger — debits and credits — but track care, time, and dignity instead of money, exposing who's actually in surplus and who's in deficit.
3. **The job interview that goes wrong** — A character is asked in an interview to explain what their current job actually does, and can't, in front of an interviewer whose own job is equally hollow.

## Reading-circle questions

1. Do you believe your own job, or one close to you, produces something real — and who decided what "real" means in that judgement?
2. Graeber argued debt is a technology of control, not a neutral fact of economics. Where do you see debt used to keep people compliant in Aotearoa — student loans, mortgages, hire-purchase?
3. Why does the market pay caregiving and cleaning so little when almost everyone agrees they matter more than most white-collar work?
4. Graeber saw direct action as the revolution in miniature, not a rehearsal for one. What's the closest thing to that in your own workplace or community right now?
5. How does Aotearoa's housing debt crisis connect to colonisation and land alienation — who was put in debt to buy back what was taken from them?

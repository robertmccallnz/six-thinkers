# Kropotkin: Mutual Aid as Method

## The thinker in one paragraph

Pyotr Kropotkin was a Russian prince who gave up his title, his inheritance, and his comfort to become one of the clearest anarchist thinkers of the nineteenth century. He trained as a geographer in Siberia, and what he saw in the animal world convinced him that the Darwinists selling "survival of the fittest" as brutal competition had missed half the story: species that cooperate survive better than species that only compete. *Mutual Aid: A Factor of Evolution* was his answer to social Darwinism dressed up as science, and it's aged better than most nineteenth-century political theory because you can test it against your own street. He wasn't naive about conflict or hierarchy — he spent years in prison for his politics and knew exactly what state power does to people who threaten it. But he insisted that cooperation isn't a fragile add-on to human nature, it's the baseline, and competition is the thing that has to be manufactured and enforced. Don't mistake him for a utopian dreamer. He was making an empirical argument, and it's one worth testing against your own tenants' union or marae before you accept or reject it.

## The dialogue seed

**Kropotkin and Graeber, on the care economy, 2026.**

KROPOTKIN: You spent a career proving that most work is bullshit. I spent mine proving that the work people do for free — feeding each other, minding each other's kids, building each other's houses — is the actual engine of survival. We're saying the same thing from different ends.

GRAEBER: Not quite the same thing. I want to know why society pays the caregiver nothing and the derivatives trader millions. That's not an oversight, Pyotr. That's the point of the system — bullshit jobs exist precisely because useless work performed for capital pays better than necessary work performed for people.

KROPOTKIN: Then the caregiver should stop waiting for the market to notice her and build the structure that pays her in kind — childcare co-ops, food networks, tool libraries. Mutual aid doesn't ask capitalism's permission.

GRAEBER: Sure, but co-ops don't pay the rent in a housing market this broken. You can build every mutual aid network you like and the landlord still wants five hundred a week.

KROPOTKIN: Then mutual aid has to include rent strikes and tenant unions, not just soup kitchens. Cooperation was never supposed to be gentle. It was supposed to be how the many outlast the few who extract from them.

## Writing prompts

1. **Essay** — Describe one mutual aid network you've actually seen work (a marae feeding people, a tool library, a tenants' WhatsApp group) and argue for why it survives without a boss.
2. **Scene** — Write two neighbours setting up an informal childcare swap, and the moment one of them realises this is political, not just practical.
3. **Letter** — Write a letter from a rest home worker to the minister responsible for aged care funding, using Kropotkin's cooperation-versus-competition frame.
4. **Monologue** — Voice someone who grew up believing "everyone for themselves" was just human nature, and describe the moment they watched a community prove otherwise.
5. **List-piece** — List every form of unpaid cooperative labour that keeps your own household or street functioning, and put a dollar figure next to each one the market refuses to pay.

## Dialogue frameworks

1. **The favour ledger** — Two characters keep score of favours done and owed, until one refuses to keep counting. Good for essays or scenes probing where mutual aid tips into obligation.
2. **The council of neighbours** — A street or building holds an informal meeting to solve a problem no landlord or council will fix. Useful for testing what self-organisation actually looks like on the page.
3. **The argument overheard on the bus** — Two strangers debate whether people are naturally selfish or naturally cooperative, without ever agreeing, while the world outside the window quietly proves one of them right.

## Reading-circle questions

1. What's one thing in your own life that survives purely on mutual aid rather than money — and what would happen to it if it had to turn a profit?
2. Kropotkin argued cooperation, not competition, is the evolutionary baseline. Does that match what you see in your workplace?
3. Where do you see mutual aid already organised in Aotearoa — kōhanga reo, food banks, tenants' unions, marae-based civil defence?
4. Is mutual aid enough on its own, or does it need to be paired with confrontation — rent strikes, union action — to actually shift power?
5. How did colonisation attack existing Māori structures of collective care and land stewardship, and what does rebuilding mutual aid look like on stolen land?

## Further reading

- [Pyotr Kropotkin, Mutual Aid: A Factor of Evolution](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/petr-kropotkin-mutual-aid-a-factor-of-evolution) — the full text, free to read.
- [Pyotr Kropotkin, The Conquest of Bread](https://theanarchistlibrary.org/library/petr-kropotkin-the-conquest-of-bread) — his practical vision of a cooperative economy.
- Rebecca Solnit, *A Paradise Built in Hell* — mutual aid after disaster, a modern companion to Kropotkin's evidence.
- David Graeber and David Wengrow, *The Dawn of Everything* — challenges the same social-Darwinist story Kropotkin was arguing against, a century apart.
